About

Building meaningful systems with curiosity and range

I am a biomedical engineering engineering student with interests in medical devices, prototyping, and artificial intelligence. I’ve always had an interest in the medical field but decided the traditional route of medicine wasn’t for me, and I strive to help others through the development of improved lab and medical equipment. During the school year and summers, I enjoy working as a lab assistant at the Engineering Product Innovation Center (EPIC) on BU campus. I’ve been able to receive training on all the equipment and now help out other students and work on projects in the shop. I also have worked as a Learning Assistant for the Mechanical Engineering department, which has instilled both a greater understanding of mechanics and a love for teaching. I love working on personal projects within STEM during my free time, and I hope you will enjoy checking out some of my favorite projects from classes, work, and my own imagination.

Guided Tour

Sierra's Engineering Journey

A deeper walkthrough of the projects that shaped how I connect artificial intelligence, medical technology, mechanical design, hands-on fabrication, electronics, and teaching.

AI became a way to imagine helping patients.

My early curiosity about artificial intelligence first clicked through AI4ALL, where I explored chest X-ray classification. It gave me a concrete bridge between code, medical data, and my long-standing interest in helping people through healthcare.

Computer vision made medical robotics feel real.

My internship at Medtronic expanded that AI thread into surgical robotics. Working around 6D pose estimation showed me how algorithms can support physical systems in high-stakes medical settings.

Then I wanted to build systems people could wear and use.

BlueGait moved me from algorithms into rehabilitation hardware. Designing sensor modules, feedback, and housings helped me think about medical devices as human-centered systems.

Medical electronics gave the device work another layer.

The EMG system taught me how muscle signals can be amplified, filtered, interpreted, and translated into mechanical output. It made the electrical-to-mechanical bridge feel tangible.

Mechanics, CAD, and fabrication became part of how I think.

Taking mechanics and working at EPIC helped me discover how much I enjoy physical design. CAD, machining, rapid prototyping, and shop work gave me the hands-on confidence to turn ideas into objects.

Teaching became its own kind of engineering challenge.

As a Learning Assistant, I found a love for helping students connect with mechanics. I learned to think about how different people approach problems and how to make difficult material feel exciting, approachable, and worth sticking with.
